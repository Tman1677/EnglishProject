Why expand into space?

The way I see it humanity has three possibilities as our technology increases and we embark into the future: our birth rates never decrease and we our unable to keep up with the growing population; our birth rates will settle, our population will plateau and we’ll settle into a sort of utopia; society collapses into a dystopia. All three of those scenarios lead to spreading out into the galaxy all for their own reasons. If the population becomes uncontrollable the logical solution is to spread the population off to other planets. If we settle into a utopia there will be very little other goals for humanity to achieve so eventually spreading out seems like a very logical goal. If society becomes a dystopia, assuming there has been time to develop decent starships, in order to escape the dystopia it seems logical at least some people will escape to a distant system. In any direction the future takes us, provided we get a decent amount more technology, I see us expanding throughout the galaxy.

Limits on Space Travel

As our current understanding of physics lays out, our universe has a “cosmic speed limit” at the speed of light. This makes long distance travel a bit trickier as any sort of interstellar travel takes an extraordinary amount of time. Even the nearest star to us, Proxima Centauri, would take us over 4.22 years even with the best possible acceleration and ignoring any side effects that extreme acceleration might have on a person, so we can safely say that it’s quite a long way to even our closest neighbor in the galaxy. and will undoubtedly require some form of propulsion that we currently just don’t possess, but it’s not completely unreasonable to assume that in the relatively near future something seemingly firmly in the realm of science fiction, such as ion drives, might become the norm.

Limits on Communication

Now, most people know and at least somewhat understand the speed of light, but one of the biggest things people tend to overlook when thinking about travelling to other planets is that all forms of communication known to us are also affected by this universal speed limit of sorts. In today’s modern era of information, with broadband internet and high speed cell towers, we have access to practically all the information in the world almost instantaneously. But, all of our methods of data transfer and communication can only go at the speed of light and no faster. On Earth this is a problem of almost no significance to the average person, after all a web server in Europe might take a little bit longer to access than one in the US but when it comes to differences in travel times for data right now we’re generally speaking in discrepancies of just a few milliseconds travelling across the continents. In most of these on-earth cases the speed of light isn’t even the main factor in slowing our data down. Once we leave our planet, the travel time of data quickly becomes an issue. Even someone as near as the moon faces a delay of 2.6 seconds for every communication. Although that may not seem like a lot I promise you that is a frustratingly long load time compared to today’s near instantaneous processing of web pages. As the distance increases the problem gets greater and greater, although most of us can accept a 39 day travel time to get to Mars - which is what it currently takes us - the over 13 minute delay for communications (at the best part of the year) would make most of us give up on casual use of the internet. When you take into account the at least four years it would take information to travel to even the nearest star system, communication across the galaxy begins to look like a hopeless endeavor. Barring some dramatic change in technology humans outside our solar system might be in effect cut off from the internet.

Possible Advancements into Space Travel

We're just one more fuel efficient form of space propulsion away from being able to go essentially anywhere we want in the universe. Although it might take years from our perspective in order to get somewhere, thanks to the wonders of relativity, if we could get an engine that accellerates at the speed of gravity we could travel the whole galaxy in one lifetime.

The Really Theoretical Stuff

The first, and original, scientifically sound theory for faster than light travel is that of Wormholes. The idea is pretty basic, and common throughout science fiction, but the general premise is that you enter at one location in space-time and come out the other, a little bit like a portal, teleporter, or any other science fiction name for it (Thorne). Kinda surprisingly, the math is theoretically sound around wormholes and they could exist, if not naturally then manmade, but they rely on the existence of exotic matter with negative mass to work. The second leading, more recent, theory is that of the Alcubierre drive, thought up by the very same Miguel Alcubierre from a paragraph ago (Cramer). This one is a bit harder to wrap your head around, but the general idea is that one would shrink space-time in front of the spacecraft and expand it behind the spacecraft to in effect travel faster than the speed of light, without defying relativity by actually travelling faster than the speed of light. The main problem is that in order to make this theoretical drive not theoretical we need, you guessed it, exotic matter with negative mass! So essentially, unless the way to travel faster than the speed of light is something we haven’t thought up yet, the key to FTL travel is exotic matter. Regardless of how we do it, any form of FTL travel, especially one that is instant, would dramatically change the future human society, making the entire universe seem a whole lot smaller.

Theoretical Communication

One of the most easily imaginable ways to improve our galaxy wide civilization would be a better form of communication, possibly even an instantaneous one. Excitingly enough, scientists have proven that data can in fact travel instantaneously through a process known as quantum entanglement. The general idea is that two paired particles can react off one another nearly instantaneously, defying the universal speed limit imposed by the theory of relativity. The only barrier to allowing us to communicate faster than light via this method is the fact that it doesn’t work. Essentially, although we can influence a particle immediately across any distance there is no way for us to control what our influence does to it. But I remain hopeful in this endeavor, after all we wouldn’t be truly looking into the future if we didn’t consider technology we do not have. Historically we find out something is possible before we can physically achieve it. The early designs for planes were based off of, and inspired by, birds; photosynthesis gave us the idea of solar energy. The way I see it, now that we have seen that some information can truly travel faster than the speed of light I think it’s only a matter of time before we unravel the secrets of instantaneous communication.
